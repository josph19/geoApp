Sphere

History
Formulae
Inventor

The sphere is one of the most perfect geometric shapes in existence. The concept of a sphere has been known since ancient times.

In ancient Greece, the sphere was considered a perfect shape by philosophers like Plato. It represented completeness and unity in many cultures. The understanding of spheres advanced greatly with the development of calculus in the 17th century, particularly through the work of mathematicians like Newton and Leibniz.

Archimedes made significant discoveries about the sphere, calculating its volume and surface area in his work "On the Sphere and Cylinder" around 225 BCE. This was one of the greatest mathematical achievements of ancient times.

Volume
V = (4/3) × π × r³
Where r is the radius of the sphere.
Surface Area
A = 4 × π × r²
Where r is the radius of the sphere.
Diameter
d = 2 × r
Where r is the radius of the sphere.

The sphere as a concept wasn't "invented" by any single person, as it is a naturally occurring shape found throughout nature. However, the mathematical properties of the sphere were studied extensively by ancient Greek mathematicians.

Archimedes (287-212 BCE) is often credited with the most important early work on the mathematical properties of spheres. His treatise "On the Sphere and Cylinder" established many fundamental properties and formulas related to spheres that we still use today.

Euclid also defined the sphere in his work "Elements" as the solid generated when a semicircle is rotated around its diameter.

Cube

Formulae

The cube is one of the five Platonic solids and has been known since ancient times. In ancient Greece, Plato associated the cube with the element earth, believing it represented stability and solidity.

Cubes have been used throughout human history for various purposes, from architecture and building blocks to game dice. The regular hexahedron (cube) was extensively studied by ancient mathematicians including Euclid and Plato.

Ancient Egyptians and Babylonians were familiar with cubes and used cubic measurements for volume. They also used cubic structures in architecture and design.

Volume
V = a³
Where a is the length of one edge of the cube.
Surface Area
A = 6 × a²
Where a is the length of one edge of the cube.
Diagonal
d = a × √3
Where a is the length of one edge of the cube.

Like the sphere, the cube wasn't invented by any one person. It's a fundamental geometric shape that has been understood for thousands of years.

The ancient Greek mathematician Pythagoras and his followers were particularly interested in cubes and other regular polyhedra. They studied the mathematical properties of these shapes extensively.

Plato described the cube as one of the five regular solids in his dialogue "Timaeus," and associated it with the element earth. These five solids later came to be known as the Platonic solids.

Cylinder

History
Formulae

The cylinder is a fundamental three-dimensional shape that has been recognized and used by humans for thousands of years. Ancient civilizations used cylindrical shapes in architecture, pottery, and engineering.

The mathematical study of cylinders began with ancient Greek mathematicians. Archimedes made significant contributions to understanding the properties of cylinders in his work "On the Sphere and Cylinder," where he derived formulas for both surface area and volume.

Cylinders have been essential in human civilization, from ancient pottery wheels and columns in Greek architecture to modern industrial applications like pipes, tanks, and engines. The Romans extensively used cylindrical columns in their architectural marvels.

Volume
V = π × r² × h
Where r is the radius of the base and h is the height of the cylinder.
Curved Surface Area
A = 2 × π × r × h
Where r is the radius and h is the height of the cylinder.
Total Surface Area
A = 2πr(r + h)
Including both circular bases and the curved surface.

The cylinder, like other basic geometric shapes, wasn't invented by a single person but has been known and used throughout human history. However, its mathematical properties were first systematically studied by ancient Greek mathematicians.

Archimedes (287-212 BCE) made the most significant early contributions to understanding cylinders. His work established the fundamental formulas for calculating the volume and surface area of cylinders that we still use today.

Euclid also mentioned cylinders in his geometric treatises, defining them as solids formed by rotating a rectangle around one of its sides. The practical applications of cylindrical shapes have made them indispensable in engineering and architecture throughout history.

Cone

Formulae
Inventor

The cone is a three-dimensional geometric shape that has been recognized since ancient times. Natural cone shapes appear in many forms, from volcanic cones to pine cones, making it a familiar shape to early civilizations.

Ancient Egyptians and Greeks were among the first to study cones mathematically. The cone was particularly important in ancient architecture and engineering, used in the construction of pyramids and various architectural elements.

The mathematical properties of cones were extensively studied by ancient Greek mathematicians, particularly Apollonius of Perga (c. 262-190 BCE), who wrote extensively about conic sections - the curves formed when a plane intersects a cone.

Volume
V = (1/3) × π × r² × h
Where r is the radius of the base and h is the height of the cone.
Curved Surface Area
A = π × r × l
Where r is the radius and l is the slant height of the cone.
Total Surface Area
A = πr(r + l)
Including both the base and the curved surface, where l is the slant height.
Slant Height
l = √(r² + h²)
Using the Pythagorean theorem, where r is radius and h is height.

The cone as a geometric shape has been known since ancient times and wasn't invented by any single person. However, its mathematical study began with ancient Greek mathematicians.

Apollonius of Perga (c. 262-190 BCE) is most famous for his work on conic sections, earning him the title "The Great Geometer." His treatise "Conics" extensively studied the properties of curves formed by intersecting cones with planes.

Earlier, Archimedes also studied cones and established formulas for their volume and surface area. The practical applications of conical shapes have been used throughout history in architecture, engineering, and art.

Square

History
Formulae
Inventor

The square is one of the most fundamental shapes in geometry and has been recognized since the dawn of human civilization. Ancient cultures used squares in art, architecture, and religious symbolism.

In ancient Egypt, squares were essential in construction and land measurement. The Egyptians used square grids for planning their pyramids and temples. Ancient Chinese culture associated the square with earth and stability in contrast to the circle representing heaven.

Greek mathematicians, particularly Euclid, provided the first formal geometric definitions of squares in his work "Elements" around 300 BCE. The Pythagoreans were fascinated by squares and their relationship to perfect numbers and geometric harmony.

Area
A = a²
Where a is the length of one side of the square.
Perimeter
P = 4 × a
Where a is the length of one side of the square.
Diagonal
d = a × √2
Where a is the length of one side of the square.
Radius of Inscribed Circle
r = a/2
The radius of the largest circle that fits inside the square.

The square, being a fundamental geometric shape, wasn't invented by any single person but has been recognized and used by humans throughout history.

Euclid (c. 300 BCE) provided the first formal mathematical definition of a square in his work "Elements," defining it as a quadrilateral with four equal sides and four right angles.

The mathematical properties of squares were studied by many ancient mathematicians, including Pythagoras, who discovered the famous theorem relating the sides of a right triangle. The Pythagorean theorem is closely connected to squares, as it states that the square of the hypotenuse equals the sum of squares of the other two sides.

Rectangle

History
Formulae
Inventor

The rectangle is one of the most commonly used shapes in human civilization. From ancient architecture to modern design, rectangles have been fundamental in construction, art, and daily life.

Ancient civilizations recognized the practical value of rectangles in building and land division. Egyptian, Mesopotamian, and Greek architects extensively used rectangular shapes in their constructions due to their structural stability and ease of construction.

The golden rectangle, with its specific proportions based on the golden ratio (φ = 1.618...), was particularly revered by ancient Greeks and Renaissance artists for its aesthetically pleasing properties. This special rectangle appears in famous works like the Parthenon and many classical paintings.

Area
A = l × w
Where l is the length and w is the width of the rectangle.
Perimeter
P = 2(l + w)
Where l is the length and w is the width of the rectangle.
Diagonal
d = √(l² + w²)
Using the Pythagorean theorem, where l is length and w is width.

The rectangle, like other basic geometric shapes, has been known and used by humans since ancient times and wasn't invented by any single person.

Euclid provided the formal mathematical definition of rectangles in his "Elements," describing them as parallelograms with four right angles. His work established the foundation for studying rectangular properties mathematically.

The practical applications of rectangles have made them indispensable throughout history. From ancient Egyptian architecture to modern computer screens, rectangles continue to be one of the most useful shapes in human design and engineering.

Isosceles Triangle

History
Formulae
Inventor

The triangle is one of the most fundamental shapes in geometry and has been studied since ancient times. Triangles are the simplest polygon and form the basis for understanding more complex geometric shapes.

Ancient civilizations recognized the structural strength of triangles. Egyptian pyramids, one of humanity's greatest architectural achievements, are essentially triangular structures. The triangle's inherent stability made it crucial in early engineering and construction.

The isosceles triangle, with two equal sides, was particularly studied by ancient Greek mathematicians. Its symmetrical properties made it important in both theoretical geometry and practical applications like architecture and art.

Area (using base and height)
A = (1/2) × b × h
Where b is the base and h is the height of the triangle.
Perimeter
P = a + b + c
Where a, b, and c are the lengths of the three sides.
Area (Heron's Formula)
A = √[s(s-a)(s-b)(s-c)]
Where s = (a+b+c)/2 is the semi-perimeter.
Isosceles Triangle Height
h = √(a² - (b/2)²)
Where a is the equal side length and b is the base.

Triangles, being fundamental geometric shapes, weren't invented by any single person but have been recognized and studied throughout human history.

Thales of Miletus (c. 624-546 BCE) is often credited with some of the first formal theorems about triangles, including the theorem that states the angles in a triangle sum to 180 degrees.

Pythagoras (c. 570-495 BCE) made perhaps the most famous contribution to triangle mathematics with the Pythagorean theorem, which relates the sides of right triangles. His work laid the foundation for much of modern trigonometry and geometry.

Circle

History
Formulae
Inventor

The circle is one of the most perfect and fundamental shapes in geometry, representing completeness and infinity in many cultures. Circles appear naturally in many forms, from the sun and moon to ripples in water.

Ancient civilizations were fascinated by circles. The Egyptians used circular measurements in astronomy and architecture. Ancient Greeks considered the circle to be the most perfect shape, and it played a central role in their geometry and philosophy.

The mathematical constant π (pi), which represents the ratio of a circle's circumference to its diameter, has been studied for thousands of years. Ancient mathematicians like Archimedes made remarkable approximations of π, and the quest to calculate π more precisely continues even today.

Area
A = π × r²
Where r is the radius of the circle.
Circumference
C = 2 × π × r
Where r is the radius of the circle.
Diameter
d = 2 × r
Where r is the radius of the circle.
Arc Length
L = (θ/360°) × 2πr
Where θ is the central angle in degrees and r is the radius.

The circle, like other fundamental geometric shapes, wasn't invented by any single person but has been recognized and studied throughout human history.

Thales of Miletus (c. 624-546 BCE) is credited with some of the earliest formal theorems about circles, including the theorem that any angle inscribed in a semicircle is a right angle.

Archimedes (287-212 BCE) made groundbreaking contributions to circle mathematics, including methods for calculating π and formulas for the area and circumference of circles. His work "Measurement of a Circle" established many principles we still use today.

Euclid also extensively studied circles in his "Elements," providing formal definitions and proofs for many circle-related theorems that form the foundation of modern geometry.
